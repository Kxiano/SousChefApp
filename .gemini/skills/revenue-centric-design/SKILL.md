# Revenue-Centric Design

A distilled playbook of **101 principles** from **Richard ([@richardrx](https://x.com/richardrx), "Design for startups")** —
a product designer specializing in conversion-rate optimization, churn reduction, and applied
behavioral science (ex-Volkswagen, PayPal, IBM). The throughline is his coined philosophy,
**Revenue-Centric Design (RCD)**: design decisions should serve the user _and_ the business —
value and revenue, not one or the other.

Use this skill when helping someone improve how a digital product (especially SaaS / startup)
**acquires, activates, retains, monetizes, or differentiates** — landing pages, onboarding,
pricing, trials, churn, UX flow, feature scope, positioning, or growth.

1. Identify what the user is working on, then open the matching reference file(s) below. Each is a

2. Ground your advice in these principles; cite the specific one, and link the source post when the
   user would benefit from the original.
3. Lead with the **named mechanism** (e.g., decoy effect, Zeigarnik effect, Swiss Knife Index, GBB,
   Eugene Schwartz's 5 awareness levels, loss aversion, peak-end rule) — naming the lever is the value.
4. Stay evidence-led: make recommendations backed by behavioral science or conversion data, not
   personal taste.

## Core Principles

### Acquisition & Landing Pages

- **Above-the-fold clarity**: Visitors decide within 3 seconds. The headline must state who you help,
  what problem you solve, and why you're different. No jargon, no metaphors.
- **Social proof ladder**: Start with logos, add testimonials with specifics (numbers, names, roles),
  escalate to case studies. Each rung reduces a different type of doubt.
- **Specificity over superlatives**: "Saves 4 hours/week" beats "saves time". Concrete numbers are
  memorable and credible.
- **CTA singularity**: One primary action per page. Multiple CTAs split attention and reduce conversions.
- **Loss framing on CTAs**: "Start saving 4 hours/week" beats "Sign up for free". Anchor to the outcome.

### Pricing & Monetization

- **Good-Better-Best (GBB)**: Three tiers using the decoy effect. The middle tier is the real target;
  the top exists to make middle look reasonable.
- **Anchoring**: Show the high price first. Everything else feels cheaper relative to it.
- **Eliminate free plans**: Free users convert at <3%. They inflate support costs and pull roadmap toward
  non-paying use cases. Replace with a time-limited trial or a money-back guarantee.
- **Make pricing visible**: Put "Pricing" in the nav. Hidden pricing increases bounce rate — visitors
  assume you're too expensive.
- **One-time vs subscription**: One-time payments overcome subscription fatigue and are 10x easier to sell.
  Only use subscriptions if recurring value justifies it architecturally.

### Onboarding & Activation

- **Time-to-value (TTV)**: The first "aha moment" must happen within the first session. Reduce every step
  between signup and the moment the user gets real value.
- **Progressive disclosure**: Show only what's needed for the current step. Reveal complexity as the user
  advances.
- **Zeigarnik effect**: Incomplete tasks create tension. Use progress bars and completion states to drive
  users forward through onboarding.
- **Show before they pay**: Put your best feature on the landing page as an interactive demo. Let people
  play before they pay.

### Retention & Churn

- **Peak-end rule**: Users remember the peak experience and the last interaction. Make the best moment
  unforgettable and every exit graceful.
- **Habit loops**: Embed the product into an existing workflow. The habit is the moat.
- **Proactive churn prevention**: Detect low-engagement signals early (login frequency, feature usage).
  Trigger human outreach before the cancel decision.
- **Hard paywall test**: If nobody converts to paid, you don't have product-market fit — you have a
  feature people like for free.

### Copy & Messaging

- **Steal copy from customers**: Customer reviews contain the exact language your audience uses. Mine
  them for headlines, benefit statements, and objection-handlers.
- **Awareness levels (Schwartz)**: Match copy to where the buyer is in awareness: Unaware → Problem
  Aware → Solution Aware → Product Aware → Most Aware. Pitching the solution to an unaware audience
  fails.
- **Emotional headline**: Make people laugh, say "wow," or think "what the f*** is this?" Features
  are forgotten; feelings are remembered.
- **Fifth-grader test**: If a 10-year-old can't understand the headline, rewrite it.

### Positioning & Differentiation

- **Category design**: Don't compete in an existing category. Name and own a new one.
- **ICP precision**: The narrower the ICP (ideal customer profile), the stronger the resonance. "For
  restaurant owners" beats "for businesses."
- **Founder visibility**: People buy from people. A 2-minute Loom from the founder beats a polished
  promo video. Show your face.
- **Ride the wave**: Build around trends, technologies, and conversations already happening. The wave
  does half the marketing.

### Feature Discipline

- **Swiss Knife Index**: The more things your product does, the less people remember it for any one
  thing. Ruthlessly cut until you're known for one thing.
- **Roadmap filter**: Every feature request passes this test: does this bring in new revenue, or
  prevent churn? If neither, deprioritize.
